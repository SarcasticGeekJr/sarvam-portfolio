Finding the Deal Blocker No One Was Tracking

How a 4-source CI audit surfaced a hidden enterprise revenue blocker and secured roadmap priority.

TrainerCentral's enterprise pipeline was growing but late-stage deals were stalling — and no one had a systematic view of why. I built a CI engine aggregating signals from four sources simultaneously: G2 and Capterra review patterns, Reddit LMS community discussions, support ticket themes, and CRM lost-deal notes. A pattern surfaced across all four that no single source would have shown alone.

SCORM compliance was the blocker. Corporate L&D buyers — the core enterprise ICP — needed to import existing training content built in Articulate 360 and Adobe Captivate. Every major competitor (Docebo, Cornerstone, TalentLMS) had native SCORM. Without it, content migration wasn't possible and deals couldn't close. The signal was consistent enough across G2 reviews, direct support requests, and CRM loss notes to quantify: multiple six-figure deals were stalled or lost against this single gap.

I built a formal business case: competitive landscape analysis showing SCORM as a universal enterprise expectation, triangulated signal volume, estimated blocked pipeline value, and a phased roadmap proposal. Presented to the product team. SCORM was prioritised within one quarter and is now in beta rollout — reaching out first to the enterprise accounts that had flagged it.

Rebuilding Pricing to Match How Enterprise Customers Actually Buy

Feature caps, cohort testing, and an enterprise tier designed before it launched.

The original flat pricing had no feature caps — power users were sitting on entry-level plans with no natural pressure to upgrade. There was also no pricing signal strong enough to trigger enterprise conversations. I started with a structured research phase: customer interviews to map real usage patterns and upgrade trigger points, CRM usage data to see where customers were outgrowing their plans, and a competitor pricing teardown to understand where we were leaving money on the table.

The output was a pricing architecture with three interventions. First, feature caps at key usage thresholds created clear value gaps between tiers. Second, a new mid-tier plan was designed and cohort-tested with existing customers before full rollout — their feedback shaped which caps to set and which features to hold back. Third, an enterprise-adjacent plan was designed with exclusive capabilities (SAML SSO, field-level encryption, advanced analytics, dedicated CSM) — not yet publicly live, but used as a deliberate conversation tool in late-stage enterprise deals where prospects asked about capabilities.

Revenue lifted 18% across two quarters post-rollout, driven by both new conversions to mid-tier and upgrade pressure on existing power users.
